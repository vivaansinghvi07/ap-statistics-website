Scope of Inference and Data Ethics
  • If the study includes a random sample, you are able to generalize the results to the population
  • If the study is a randomly-assigned experiment, you can determine causation
  • Since some experiments are morally questionable, they lack realism and therefore lack a clear answer
    • However, given certain circumstances, you are able to make assumptions about causation from an observational study
      • The association must be strong
      • The association must be consistent
      • Larger values of the explanatory variable lead to larger values of the response variable
      • The cause precedes the effect
      • The conclusion is plausible
    • For example, it is not morally right to conduct an experiment about smoking related to lung cancer
      • However, there exists enough research and data to meet the conditions listed above, so you can reasonably assume smoking leads to lung cancer despite the lack of an experiment
  • An important thing to consider when making an experiment is data ethics:
    • Studies must be reviewed in advance by an institutional review board (IRB), which makes sure the experiment is safe and plausible
    • All individuals participating in the study must have informed consent
      • They must be willing to participate AND know what they are participating in
    • All personally identifiable data must be kept completely confidential
      • Specific medical records and other personal information are all identifiable (you are able to link them to a person)
